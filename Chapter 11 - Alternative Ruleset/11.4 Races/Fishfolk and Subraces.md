## Nerid
Nerids have a human upper body with a fish tail from the hips downward when in water. The upper skeleton follows a human plan: upright spine, broad ribcage, two arms with normal shoulders and elbows, and hands with five fingers with some webbing in between each finger. The lower body is a long, muscular tail built for steady thrust. The tail is thick at the base, narrows gradually, and ends in a broad fin. The fin can be split into an upper and lower lobe, or be a single wide fan, but it is always stiff enough to push water with strong strokes. Along the sides of the tail, small stabilizing fins may appear as low ridges.

They have gills placed along the neck and upper ribs, usually as clean slits that open wider during hard swimming. The gill tissue is moist and darker than the surrounding skin. Nerids can breathe air with lungs and water with gills, and the throat and chest often move in a slow, strong rhythm when they are submerged. Scales change with moisture. When fully underwater, the entire body becomes scaled: small, overlapping plates that catch light like fine armor. When dry, most scales fade or retract into the skin so the upper body looks closer to human skin, with only patches of scale left at the shoulders, outer arms, hips, and along the spine. Any area that gets wet shows scale definition again, starting as faint texture and becoming full plates as the skin stays soaked.

When out of water, the tail converts into legs. The legs are long and thin compared to a human of the same height, with less visible muscle bulk and a slightly unstable look when first formed. The skin of the legs remains partly scaly, especially on the shins, calves, and feet. Feet are narrow, with slightly webbed toes and tough, smooth soles. Their face is strongly human in shape and symmetry, but the mouth holds sharper teeth than a human. The front teeth are narrow and pointed, and the canines are longer, built for gripping slippery prey. Eyes are large, clear, and glossy, with bright irises; lids close horizontally instead of vertically like most humanoids and can seal tightly against water. Hair can be any human-like color, but it often looks heavier and wetter, clinging in strands even when not submerged. 
## Mermaid
Mermaids have the same general “human and fish” components as nerids, but their body arrangement flips depending on whether they are submerged or not. In water, the lower body is human legs and feet, while the upper body is fish-like. The fish upper body is not a full animal head; it is still built around a humanoid skull and shoulders, but the surface becomes more aquatic: smooth wet skin, heavy scaling, and fin structures along the arms, shoulders, and back. The chest and throat show gill slits more strongly than a nerid’s, often larger and more obvious, with visible folds of moist tissue. The arms remain present, but the forearms may carry thin fin-webbing that spreads when swimming.

Out of water, the upper body becomes more human while the lower body becomes a fish tail. The human upper half has normal skin texture, hair, and a standard human torso outline, though it may keep small patches of scale at the collarbones, outer shoulders, and along the sides of the neck. The fish tail is strong and heavy, with thicker scale plates than a nerid’s in many individuals, and a fin that is broader but less fine in detail. This creates an awkward contrast: a very human-looking chest and face above a clearly aquatic lower body. When partially wet, the shift can look uneven, with one side showing more scales or more fin growth than the other.

Their facial structure tends to keep a pretty symmetry, but the fish influence shows in the skin sheen, the slick look of the lips, and the way the eyes reflect light. Teeth vary: some have normal human-like teeth, while others keep narrow, sharp teeth. Hair often looks limp and water-heavy, and the scalp may show fine scale texture near the hairline when wet. 
## Cephalid
Cephalids have a human body plan in the torso and head, but their limbs are replaced by strong tentacles. The core skeleton is humanlike: spine, ribcage, shoulders, and hips are present and shaped for upright posture. The head is human in outline, with normal facial bones, lips, and nose although their ears are more of just holes on the side of their head than actual ears. The skin is slightly slick to the touch, as if it holds a thin wet film, but it is not dripping. It reflects light softly rather than shining like oil.

Instead of arms, each shoulder leads into a thick tentacle base. The tentacles are muscular tubes that taper toward the end. They can bend in any direction and can coil tightly. The surface is smooth and elastic, and the underside is lined with suction cups in rows. The cups are small near the base and can become larger mid-length, then smaller again near the tip, depending on the individual. The tentacle tips can be blunt and rounded, or split into small finger-like prongs for fine control. Legs are also tentacles, attached at the hips. These lower tentacles are thicker and built to support body weight. When moving on land, they act like flexible columns that plant, grip, and lift in short steps. In water, they spread and pull like oars, giving strong control and sudden acceleration.

Cephalids often have subtle gill slits along the neck or ribs, but they can also rely on lungs and hold breath well. Their eyes are large and clear, with a calm, focused stare. The pupils can widen dramatically in low light. Hair is normal in growth pattern but often looks heavy and damp, and many cephalids keep it short to avoid tangling with suction cups. Their mouth and teeth are usually humanlike, though some have slightly sharper canines. 
## Deepmantle
Deepmantles appear fully human from the waist up, with normal arms, hands, and head, but instead of two legs they have a dense cluster of tentacles and they do not have ears, instead having only holes. The upper skeleton is humanlike and often looks strong and well-supported, because the lower body has no rigid leg bones to brace against the ground. The shoulders, spine, and core muscles are usually very developed. The arms are normal in shape and function, ending in five-fingered hands with normal nails and grip.

From the hips downward, the body opens into multiple tentacles rather than a single tail. The number can vary, but it is always many, forming a living base that can spread wide for stability or gather tight for speed. Each tentacle is shorter than a cephalid’s main limb tentacles, but they are thicker at the base and packed closely together. They taper to narrow tips and can lift the body in a walking motion by planting several tentacles at once. On smooth ground, their movement can look too fluid, like sliding that is broken into steps only when you watch closely. In water, the tentacles fan outward and pull in coordinated waves, giving high control and fast turning.

The skin is slick and slightly cold, with a steady wet sheen. The underside of each tentacle is lined with suction cups, often smaller and more numerous than a cephalid’s. These cups can grip stone, wood, and flesh, leaving round marks if held too long. Deepmantles commonly have gill slits along the ribs or sides of the neck, and the gill openings flare during hard swimming. Their face remains human in symmetry and softness, with large eyes and smooth skin. Teeth are usually humanlike, though some have sharper points. Hair is often dark or muted in color and tends to cling together in wet strands. 
## Chelonar
Chelonars are upright turtlefolk with a heavy, armored frame. The skeleton is thick and load-bearing, with wide shoulders, a deep chest, and strong hips. They stand on two legs and have two arms, with joints that move more slowly than a human’s but hold weight and force extremely well. The hands have thick fingers, usually four or five depending on the individual, ending in blunt claws. The claws are hard keratin, more like tough nails than sharp talons. Feet are broad and flat with thick pads and short clawed toes, built for pushing through mud and gripping wet stone.

A large shell covers the back. The shell is fused to the rib structure, forming a hard dome that rises from the shoulders down over the hips. The surface is made of large plates separated by shallow seams. Plates often show rings, scratches, and worn edges from age. Shell colors tend to be earthy: deep green, olive, brown, dark gray, or mottled mixes, sometimes with lighter seams. The front of the torso is protected by a chest plate, like a solid “belly shell,” which can be smoother and slightly lighter in color. The neck can withdraw partway into the shell, and the shoulder openings are thick, forming strong armor rims around the arm joints.

The head is turtle-like: broad skull, blunt beak-like mouth, and thick skin with fine scale texture. Eyes are forward and steady, with dark pupils and irises that can be green, brown, amber, or near-black. The skin is tough and pebbled, especially on the limbs and neck, and it holds deep creases at joints. Hair is absent. Teeth are not visible in the way human teeth are; instead, the mouth edge is hard and beak-like, suited for crushing and tearing.

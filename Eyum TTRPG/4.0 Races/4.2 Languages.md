At level 1 you know Common and your racial language. If your Intelligence is greater than 14 you may learn one additional language for every 2 points of Intelligence above 14. You can also learn new languages during play through study with a willing teacher or from written texts, which typically takes several months of dedicated effort per language or through special features such as the Linguist feat (see [[1.3.3 Leveling Up]]) which grants 3 additional languages instantly. 
## Languages
### Common
Common is the trade tongue of Eyum, spoken by nearly every civilized creature to some degree. It is the default language of commerce, diplomacy, and public life.

**Spoken:** Common is a flat, efficient language with even syllables and little tonal variation. Words are spoken at a moderate pace with clear enunciation. Vowels are broad and open, consonants are crisp but not harsh. Regional accents exist but rarely impede understanding.

**Written:** The Common script uses 26 alphabetic characters written left to right in horizontal rows. Letters are composed of straight lines and simple curves. Ink on parchment or paper is standard. The script is blocky and practical, designed for speed of writing rather than ornament. Numbers are written with a decimal system using ten distinct symbols.
### Bugish
Spoken by Bugfolk and all bugfolk subraces.

**Spoken:** Bugish is a chittering, percussive language built from rapid clicking sounds produced by mandible and palatal strikes, supplemented with hissing sibilants and low droning hums from the thorax. Vowels are brief and clipped. The language has no labial sounds since bugfolk mouths are not structured for lip closure. Speakers punctuate statements with antennal movements that carry grammatical meaning, meaning Bugish spoken by non-bugfolk is often missing subtle inflections. The language sounds to outsiders like rapid sticks tapping together mixed with soft whistles.

**Written:** Bugish is written in two scripts. The primary script uses geometric symbols composed of dots, dashes, and circles arranged in grids, read top to bottom in vertical columns. Each symbol represents a word or concept rather than a sound. The secondary script, used for phonetic transcription of names and loanwords, uses a set of angular glyphs based on leg joint angles, written left to right. Traditional Bugish texts are engraved on chitin plates or pressed into soft clay.
### Infernal
Spoken by Demons and all demon subraces.

**Spoken:** Infernal is a guttural, resonant language spoken from deep in the throat. Words are built from hard consonants, rolled r's, and open vowels that carry a natural echo when spoken aloud. The language has three registers of formality that affect vowel length and pitch. In casual speech Infernal sounds rough and abrupt. In formal or ritual contexts the same words become drawn out and melodic, almost chant-like. 

**Written:** The Infernal script is carved, never written with ink. Characters are angular runes composed entirely of straight lines meeting at sharp angles, with no curves. The script is read right to left in horizontal rows. Each rune represents a syllable. Letters are never connected. Traditional Infernal texts are etched into stone, metal, or bone. The script's sharp lines make it unsuitable for parchment.
### Elvish
Spoken by Elves and all elven subraces.

**Spoken:** Elvish is a flowing, vowel-rich language spoken with a light breathiness. Consonants are soft and never plosive. The language has a natural musicality, with pitch rising and falling in gentle waves across sentences. Speakers use subtle breath control to extend vowels, giving Elvish a lilting quality. There are seventeen vowel sounds and twelve consonants. The language has no harsh or guttural sounds. 

**Written:** The Elvish script is a flowing cursive of connected loops and sweeping curves, written left to right. Characters are designed to be written rapidly without lifting the quill. The script is highly ornamental, with ascenders and descenders that extend far above and below the baseline. Letters change form based on their position in a word. Elvish texts are traditionally written on vellum with oak-gall ink. The script is considered the most beautiful of the mortal languages and is frequently used in decorative inscriptions.
### Fishish
Spoken by Fishfolk and all fishfolk subraces.

**Spoken:** Fishish is a language that relies on sounds produced underwater. It uses a combination of resonant hums, clicks, and whistles generated by vibrating a specialized membrane in the throat. Vowels are long and sonorous. Consonants are produced by tongue and teeth clicks. Pitch carries grammatical meaning, with rising pitch indicating questions and falling pitch indicating statements. Fishish is difficult for non-fishfolk to speak clearly because it requires water in the throat to produce full sounds. Above water, Fishish sounds like a series of wet clicks and low hums.

**Written:** Fishish has no native written script. Fishfolk were historically a pre-literate culture. When writing is required, they use a system of pictographs carved into smooth stone or pressed into wet clay, with images representing objects, actions, and ideas directly. The pictographs are arranged in spirals read from center outward. Modern fishfolk who live near other races typically adopt the Common script for written communication.
### Harpy
Spoken by Harpies and all harpy subraces.

**Spoken:** Harpy is a high-pitched, whistling language built from frequencies that carry over long distances. Words are formed from whistles, trills, and warbles produced by a specialized syrinx at the base of the trachea. The language has no consonants as non-harpy races would recognize them. Volume and pitch carry as much meaning as the sounds themselves. A single word can mean different things when whispered versus sung at full voice. Harpy sounds beautiful to most listeners but is almost impossible for non-harpies to replicate accurately. To humanoid ears it sounds like birdsong with deliberate structure.

**Written:** Harpy is written in a script of curved symbols that represent musical phrases rather than individual words. Each character denotes a specific pitch, duration, and volume pattern. The script is written left to right in wavy horizontal lines that mirror the pitch contour of the spoken phrase. Harpies typically scratch their writing into tree bark or soft stone using their talons. The script is difficult for non-harpies to read because it requires understanding the musical relationships between symbols rather than a fixed alphabet.
### Ophedian
Spoken by Nagas and all naga subraces.

**Spoken:** Ophedian is a sibilant language dominated by fricatives and extended hisses. The tongue is the primary articulator, producing sounds by pressing against the palate and teeth in rapid sequences. Vowels are low and throaty. The language has a natural rhythm that matches the undulating movement of a serpent's body. Speakers use subtle tongue-flicker patterns as part of grammar, indicating tense by the speed of the flicker. To non-speakers Ophedian sounds like continuous hissing punctuated by low throat tones.

**Written:** Ophedian is written in long, winding script of connected characters that flow left to right in a single continuous line, never breaking between words. The script curves and loops in patterns that resemble a serpent's path. Characters represent syllables rather than individual sounds. Traditional Ophedian texts are written on scrolls made from shed snakeskin, using inks mixed from mineral pigments. The writing has no punctuation; pauses and stops are indicated by larger loops in the characters themselves.
### Therian
Spoken by Therians and all therian subraces.

**Spoken:** Therian is a language built around growls, barks, yips, and throat rumbles produced by vocal cords built for short, powerful bursts of sound. The language has a wide pitch range, with low rumbles for declarative statements and higher barks for questions. Vowels are short and guttural. Consonants are produced through the teeth and the back of the throat. Therian uses ear and tail positions as grammatical markers, making the full language impossible to speak correctly without the appropriate anatomy. To non-speakers it sounds like animal vocalizations arranged in deliberate patterns.

**Written:** Therian is written as a series of claw-mark symbols scratched into surfaces. Each character is composed of a set number of parallel or intersecting claw lines. The number of lines and their angles determine the meaning. The script is read left to right in horizontal rows. Therians traditionally write on cured hide or bark by raking their claws across the surface. The script is angular and sparse, with each character separate from the next. Fine details are difficult to represent in claw-script, so the written language is less nuanced than the spoken form.
### Tullish
Spoken by Tulls and all tull subraces.

**Spoken:** Tullish is a deep, booming language spoken from the chest with heavy resonance. Words are built from long vowels and hard plosive consonants that carry through stone and earth. The language has a slow, deliberate cadence with noticeable pauses between words. Volume is part of grammar, softer tones indicate questions or uncertainty, full-voiced tones indicate statements of fact. Tullish has very few words for abstract concepts and a large vocabulary for physical objects, terrain features, and kinship relationships. To non-speakers it sounds like rocks grinding together.

**Written:** The Tullish script is carved in angular block letters with no curves or connecting strokes. Each letter is a separate character composed of straight lines meeting at right angles. The script is written top to bottom in vertical columns, then left to right across the page. Traditional Tullish texts are carved into stone slabs or cut into thick slabs of cured wood. The script is designed for durability rather than speed, meant to last centuries. Ink writing is rare in Tullish culture.
### Dead Speak
Spoken by Undead and all undead subraces.

**Spoken:** Dead Speak is a low, rasping language produced by forcing air through partially collapsed or damaged vocal cords. The language has no pitch variation to speak of. Every word is spoken on the same flat tone. Vowels are hollow and breathy. Consonants are dry and clicking. The language is naturally quiet, difficult to hear from more than twenty feet away. Dead Speak has no raised voice, no shouting, no whispering. Every statement is delivered at the same volume regardless of urgency or emotion. To living creatures Dead Speak sounds unsettling because it lacks the natural pitch variation that living speech has.

**Written:** The Dead Speak script is composed of dots and short dashes arranged in ordered clusters, typically scratched into bone or etched into stone with a fingernail or tool. Each cluster of marks represents a word. The script is read in horizontal rows from left to right. There is no punctuation or capitalization. The simplicity of the script reflects that it is often written by fingers alone without proper writing implements. Dead Speak texts tend to be short and direct.
### Dragonic
Spoken by dragons and certain dragon-adjacent creatures. Dragonic is not a starting language for any player race and must be learned through study, the Dragon Slayer Skill Tree or the Linguist feat.

**Spoken:** Dragonic is a deep, resonant language produced in the chest and throat with significant vocal power behind each syllable. The language uses hard consonants, rolled r's, and long vowels that can be sustained for several seconds. Pitch carries meaning, with different tones changing a word's meaning entirely. Dragonic has five distinct tones. The language also includes sub-vocal rumbles that modify meaning but are felt rather than heard, making it difficult for non-dragons to understand fully. To humanoid ears Dragonic sounds like a series of powerful growls and roars arranged in deliberate sequence.

**Written:** Dragonic has no written script.

## Health and Vitality
Hit Points (HP) are your actual health. When HP hits 0, you die (see exceptions below). HP is harder to recover and generally requires healing magic, potions, or rest. Think of HP like “death saves without the randomness”: once you’re taking HP damage, you are actively dying and should disengage or get healed.

Vitality (Vit) is your stamina and evasive buffer, it represents dodges, deflections, and near-misses. Most damage is taken from Vitality first; when Vitality reaches 0, subsequent damage spills into HP. Vitality is easier to restore via rest, potions, or abilities, and the system treats it as a resource you can spend without long-term harm.

Generally Speaking all damage is reduced from the target’s Vitality first, and then Health if Vitality reaches 0.
##### How damage interacts with Vitality vs. HP
The normal case
- Single-target weapon/spell damage: reduce Vitality first, then HP if Vitality is at 0.
	- After taking HP damage (not Vitality damage) you run the risk of injuries.
- Area-of-Effect (AoE)
	- Unless a spell/ability says otherwise, half of the AoE’s damage bypasses Vitality and hits HP directly; the other half applies to Vitality.
- Other ways damage can bypass Vitality
	- Sleeping/Unconscious target: any successful hit is a critical and bypasses Vitality, applying full damage to HP.
	- Undodgeable/inescapable situations (restrained, helpless, truly unaware): bypass Vitality; the GM judges whether the situation was genuinely unavoidable.
	- Critical hits set all damage dice to their maximum before adding modifiers and bypasses Vitality.
##### Death and Revival
At 0 HP, a creature dies instantly.
Exceptions:  
- Some feats/features can override death at 0 HP.  
- Some spells/abilities include a revival window (e.g., “revivable within 10 minutes”). If used in time, the creature can return.
Finality: after 48 hours dead, the soul is unreachable, no magic, ritual, or divine act can restore the creature.

> **So when does damage actually hurt you?**
>
> Here's how to think about Vitality vs HP. Vitality is your stamina, dodging, blocking, rolling with the punches. It comes back on a short rest. HP is your actual flesh and blood. Once you're losing HP, you're bleeding, broken, and one bad hit from death.
>
> **Normal hit:** A sword swing deals 8 damage. You have 12 Vitality left. You lose 8 Vitality trying to dodge. No big deal, next short rest, you're fine.
>
> **Same hit, you're out of Vitality:** That 8 damage goes straight to HP. Now you're injured. You need healing magic or a long rest.
>
> **Fireball (AoE):** The wizard drops a fireball that deals 24 damage. Half of that (12) bypasses Vitality and hits HP directly. The other 12 goes to Vitality. So you lose 12 HP and 12 Vit in one shot. This happens because it is unreasonable or impossible to dodge the entire effect from an aoe attack.
>
> **Crit:** An enemy crits you with a dagger. Normally it'd deal 1d4+2 (say 5 damage to Vit). Instead it deals max damage (4+2=6) straight to HP. That one lucky stab just turned a scratch into a real wound.
## Injuries
##### When to check for an Injury
You only ever check when HP is actually harmed (Vitality hits don’t trigger injuries). Check once per source of HP damage (one attack/spell/trap), using the HP portion for mixed cases. Having any injury active will prevent recovery from resting. Once all effects from the injury end you are free to rest again like normal. Some injuries can be cured by resting, in that case the partial resting is applied on this rest but not on any future rests. 
##### Massive Damage Rule
If you take ≥ 50% of your maximum HP in one hit, make a DC 10 Constitution save:
- Fail: you are unconscious until the end of your next turn (or until you take damage again).
- Succeed: you remain standing, but take -1 to all attack rolls on your next turn.
- Make sure that you apply an injury check after seeing if you are unconscious 

> Note: If the target is reduced to 0 HP, they die; do not bother with injuries.

> **How injuries actually work, step by step**
>
> Let's say you have 40 max HP and a giant smashes you for 22 damage to HP (after your Vitality is gone).
>
> **Step 1: Severity:** 22 out of 40 is 55%. That's ≥50%, so it's a Severe injury. It also triggers Massive Damage (≥50% max HP in one hit), so you need to make a DC 10 Con save or fall unconscious until the end of your next turn.
>
> **Step 2: Injury Save:** The DC for Severe is 20. Your Con save is +4. You roll a 14 + 4 = 18 which is a failure. You check the injury mapping table for whatever damage type hit you and apply the condition listed.
>
> **Step 3: Overkill:** If you had passed that save, your Overkill level would go up by 1. Next time you get injured, the DC would be higher or you'd roll with disadvantage. If you keep passing saves, Overkill stacks up to level 5, at which point you just auto-fail your next injury save. 
>
> **If it were a crit:** That same 22-damage hit from a critical would bump the severity up by one level from Severe to Extreme. So even though it's only 55% of your HP, it counts as Extreme (≥75% threshold for severity purposes). The save DC jumps from 20 to 25.
>
> And remember: while you have any injury active, you can't recover from resting properly. You only get half benefits on short and long rests until the injury is resolved.

##### Step 1 Determine Injury Severity (from that one source of HP damage)
Calculate % of max HP lost from that single source (round down any fractional HP when comparing to thresholds):

| Severity      | HP lost from a single source/attack                           |
| ------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Minor         | ≥10% and <25% of max HP                                       |
| Moderate      | ≥25% and <50% of max HP                                       |
| Severe        | ≥50% of max HP (this also triggers Massive Damage separately) |
| Extreme       | ≥75% of max HP (this also triggers Massive Damage separately) |
**Critical Hit upgrade:** When dealing damage with a critical hit the Severity rating will go up by 1 level. So if you only dealt 15% of the targets max health as damage it would still be considered a moderate injury not a minor one like normal. 
##### Step 2 Injury Save (CON)
Make a Constitution saving throw vs the DC for the severity:

| Severity  | Injury Save (CON) DC |
| --------- | -------------------- |
| Minor     | DC 10                |
| Moderate  | DC 15                |
| Severe    | DC 20                |
| Extreme   | DC 25                |
- **Success:** No injury is applied; increase your Overkill level by 1 (to a maximum of 5) (see below).
- **Failure:** apply the conditions listed in the injury mapping table (See [[2.3 Injuries]]).
- **Natural 1:** apply the conditions listed but increase their save DC's by 5 and increase the number of turns they last by 1.
###### Overkill Scaling
Each time you succeed on an Injury Save, your Overkill level increases by 1 (max 5). If you fail an Injury Save, your Overkill level resets to 0. While you have an Overkill level, you suffer the cumulative effects listed below on subsequent Injury Saves until your Overkill level changes. (Effects at higher levels include all effects from lower levels.)

| Overkill Level | Effect on subsequent Injury Saves                  |
| -------------- | -------------------------------------------------- |
| 1              | Roll your next Injury Save with disadvantage.      |
| 2              | +5 to the Injury Save DC (cumulative).             |
| 3              | +10 to the Injury Save DC (cumulative, total +15). |
| 4              | +10 to the Injury Save DC (cumulative, total +25). |
| 5              | You automatically fail your next Injury Save.      |
##### Step 3 Injury Mapping 
(by damage type → apply these Conditions on a failed Injury Save)

For a list of injuries see [[2.3 Injuries]]

> If a minor injury causes a condition, it cannot reduce your HP below 1. When this happens, the condition automatically ends once your HP reaches that point. However, moderate and higher injuries can still be fatal.

## Resting
###### Short Rest:  
A short rest is a period of downtime lasting at least 30 minutes, where your character can catch their breath, tend to wounds, etc.

During a short rest, you:
- Regain all your Vitality and 50% of your maximum mana
- Refresh certain class features that refresh on a short rest (they will be specified by the skill)
- You **DO NOT** regain any HP during a short rest
- **If you have an injury:** You only regain 50% of Vitality and Mana.
###### Long Rest:  
A long rest takes 8 hours of uninterrupted rest or sleep. During a long rest, you:
- Fully restore HP, Vitality, and Mana
- Regain expended abilities
- Clear all levels of exhaustion 
- **If you have an injury:** You only regain 50% of your max HP and 75% of Vitality and Mana.

*Optional Rule*: If your Game Master wants to, they may not allow you to take long rests when in a dungeon or exploring a forest because you won’t really be able to get good rest. In this scenario you would only be able to get a long rest when in a house or actual city with a real bed. 

Note: If a long rest is interrupted (e.g., by combat), it must be fully restarted to gain benefits.
### Exhaustion
Exhaustion represents a character's declining condition due to overexertion, starvation, lack of rest, or certain magical effects.

Exhaustion is tracked in levels from 1 to 6, with each level imposing increasingly severe penalties:

| Exhaustion Level | Effect                                         |
| ---------------- | ---------------------------------------------- |
| 1                | Disadvantage on ability checks                 |
| 2                | Speed halved                                   |
| 3                | Disadvantage on attack rolls and saving throws |
| 4                | HP and Vitality max reduced by 50%             |
| 5                | Speed reduced to 5ft                           |
| 6                | Death                                          |

**Note**: These penalties are cumulative. A character with level 3 exhaustion suffers the effects of levels 1, 2, and 3 simultaneously.

> **Yes, the exhaustion levels stack. Here's what that actually means.**
>
> At level 1, you have disadvantage on ability checks, this is annoying but manageable. At level 2, your speed is halved *on top of that*, so now you're bad at skills and slow. At level 3, you also have disadvantage on attacks and saves. Good luck fighting anything. At level 4, your max HP and Vitality are cut in half, so you're both fragile and useless. At level 5, you can move 5 ft per turn, you're basically crawling. At level 6, you die.
>
> You only remove 1 level per long rest. So if you somehow hit level 4 exhaustion, it takes four days of bed rest to fully recover. Spells that remove exhaustion are rare, treat it as a real threat, not a minor debuff.
###### Causes of Exhaustion
- Going without food, water, or sleep for extended periods of time
- Traveling for more than 16 hours without rest
- Failing certain saving throws (e.g., extreme environments or spell/potion effects)
###### Removing Exhaustion
- Long Rest: Removes **1** level of exhaustion.
- Magical Means: Some spells can remove exhaustion instantly.
## Armor Class (AC)
Armor class in Eyum represents how hard it is to be hit. When you make an attack roll against an enemy your roll is compared to their AC (armor class), if you match or exceed the AC then you hit. AC can be gained from many things from such as your Dex Mod, Armor, magic effects and more. Your AC bonus granted from your Dex mod changes depending on several factors. Your Base AC is calculated as 10 + Armor + Dex Bonus. 

| Armor            | Max Dex Mod | If Expert | Dodge Master |
| ---------------- | ----------- | --------- | ------------ |
| **No Armor**     | +4          | N/A       | +7           |
| **Light Armor**  | +5          | +7        | +7           |
| **Medium Armor** | +4          | +6        | +7           |
| **Heavy Armor**  | +3          | +5        | +7           |
The bonus from AC also changes as the number raises. You cannot gain more than +7 AC from your dexterity no matter how high it is. 

| Dex Mod | AC Bonus | \|  | Dex Mod | Ac Bonus |
| ------- | -------- | --- | ------- | -------- |
| +1      | +1       | \|  | +12     | +6       |
| +2      | +2       | \|  | +13     | +6       |
| +3      | +3       | \|  | +14     | +6       |
| +4      | +3       | \|  | +15     | +6       |
| +5      | +4       | \|  | +16     | +6       |
| +6      | +4       | \|  | +17     | +6       |
| +7      | +4       | \|  | => +18  | +7       |
| +8      | +5       | \|  |         |          |
| +9      | +5       | \|  |         |          |
| +10     | +5       | \|  |         |          |
| +11     | +5       | \|  |         |          |
> So if you aren't wearing any armor and have say 20 (+5) in Dex you would have 14 AC, if you have 12 (+2) in Dex you would have 12 AC and so on. For more information on how Armor will effect AC see [[2.5 Weapons, Armors, and Tools]].

> There is no actual cap on AC, just a cap on how much benefit your Dex can provide for your AC. Things like spells and runes can make your AC much higher than the Dex bonus will ever provide. 
###### Armored Resilience (AC in Saving Throws)
An exceptionally high Armor Class does more than just deflect attacks, it can also render you impervious to certain physical hazards. If your AC exceeds the DC by its armor type threshold (see below) of a DEX, STR, or CON saving throw, and the effect could reasonably be blocked, absorbed, or weathered by your armor (at the GM's discretion), you automatically succeed on that saving throw without needing to roll. When you pass a saving throw this way, the effect is fully negated, you take no damage and suffer no secondary effects (when wearing medium or heavy armor). This overrides the usual "half damage on a successful save" rule which some of those effects may have had. The rationale is that your armor is so robust that the hazard simply cannot penetrate, circumvent, or meaningfully affect you. Injury saves and effects from saves caused from your injury cannot be auto passed.

The armor type places some restrictions on these auto saves and the threshold (how much higher your AC must be for Armored Resilience to take effect) for blocking. The Armor Master feat (see [[Eyum TTRPG/3.0 Character Management/3.3 Leveling Up|3.3 Leveling Up]]) will modify the following thresholds to the number in (). So the format will look like: DefaultValue (ValueWithArmorMaster). 

|                       | Light  | Medium                                                   | Heavy                                                    |
| --------------------- | ------ | -------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Threshold**         | 15 (8) | 10 (5)                                                   | 5 (2)                                                    |
| **Damage Mitigation** | None   | Auto passing saves negate effects/damage instead of half | Auto passing saves negate effects/damage instead of half |
| **Saves Mitigated**   | Dex    | Dex, Con                                                 | Dex, Con, Str                                            |
**GM Guidance**: This rule should only apply when the armor itself would logically provide protection. WIS, INT, and CHA saves (mental effects, illusions, charm) are never eligible. DEX saves against explosions or AoE spells almost always qualify if the armor fully encases the body. STR saves against being moved or knocked down may apply if the armor is heavy enough to anchor the wearer. 
## Affinity
Your Affinity represents how attuned you are to a specific element or magical source. Each affinity governs a unique damage type, and monsters will be weak or resistant to different affinities depending on their type. For more information on specific affinities and their unlock conditions, see [[2.1 Affinities]].

Affinity is tracked as a raw number (e.g., 25 Fire Affinity), but that raw number is never added directly to anything. Instead, whenever a rule says to use your **Attack Roll Bonus**, **Attack Damage Bonus**, or **Save DC Bonus**, you take your affinity number and look it up on the corresponding table in [[2.1 Affinities#Affinity Bonus Tables]]. The tables use diminishing returns: more affinity still helps, but each extra point is worth less than the last.

| Term                | Used For                                  | Table                                  |
| ------------------- | ----------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------- |
| Attack Roll Bonus   | Spell attack rolls (to hit)               | [[2.1 Affinities#Attack Roll Bonus]]   |
| Attack Damage Bonus | Damage added to attacks and some features | [[2.1 Affinities#Attack Damage Bonus]] |
| Save DC Bonus       | Spell and ability save DCs                | [[2.1 Affinities#Save DC Bonus]]       |

**Note:** Base Magic Damage (sometimes called Gen Dmg Mod or Generic Damage Mod) is a separate stat that is also added to your spell damage. It is not part of the affinity lookup system. Your Gen Dmg Mod comes from leveling and other sources, not from affinity.
##### Generic Affinity
Generic Affinity is your overall magical attunement, your ability to channel energy of any type. Unlike specific affinities, it applies to all spells equally, regardless of element. Generic Affinity cannot be increased with Affinity Points (Affp) under normal circumstances. Like specific affinities, Generic Affinity uses the lookup tables: your Generic Affinity number is looked up on the Generic column of each table to find your Generic Attack Roll Bonus, Generic Attack Damage Bonus, and Generic Save DC Bonus. See [[2.1 Affinities#Affinity Bonus Tables]].
##### Specific Affinities
Specific affinities represent your connection to a particular element: Fire, Earth, Water, Air, Radiant, Necrotic, Psychic, and many advanced hybrid types. Advanced types (Lightning, Steam, Magma, Ice/Cold, etc.) have prerequisites, you must already have some points (usually 10+) in their parent affinities. Once unlocked, they can be upgraded like any other affinity. If increased artificially though a skill, feature, or level it does not unlock it. You may be able to increase it enough to use it like normal but you cannot spend affinity points in it until you have met its prerequisites. Like Generic Affinity, you look up your specific affinity number on the Standard column of each table to find your Standard Attack Roll Bonus, Standard Attack Damage Bonus, and Standard Save DC Bonus. For more information check [[2.1 Affinities]].

> **How affinities actually work in practice**
>
> Affinity splits into two parts: Generic and Specific.
>
> Generic Affinity is your baseline magical talent. You look it up on the Generic column of each table in [[2.1 Affinities#Affinity Bonus Tables]] to get your Generic Attack Roll Bonus (for spell attack rolls), Generic Attack Damage Bonus (for spell damage), and Generic Save DC Bonus (for spell save DCs). You can't buy Generic Affinity with Affinity Points (Affp) (usually). You only get it from class features, race bonuses, and the occasional feat.
>
> Specific Affinities (Fire, Earth, Water, Air, Radiant, Necrotic, Psychic, and all the advanced ones) are where you spend your Affp. You look up your specific affinity number on the Standard column of each table to get your Standard Attack Roll Bonus, Standard Attack Damage Bonus, and Standard Save DC Bonus. More Fire Affinity means higher bonuses on the tables which means your fire spells hit more often, deal more damage, and have higher save DCs.
>
> The catch with advanced affinities (Lightning, Magma, Ice/Cold, etc.) is that you need many points in their parent affinities before you can unlock them. Want Lightning spells? You need 10 Fire and 10 Air first. You can't skip straight to the cool stuff, sense each affinity is a damage type however this means your unique and powerful affinities will be harder to dodge or block as less creatures will be resistant or know how to handle them. 
>
> For the full list of what unlocks what, see [[2.1 Affinities]].
